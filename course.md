# DSPR 136 Cariology: Course Material

## Learning Objectives: Exam-Ready Answers

| Syllabus objective | Exam-ready answer | Must-memorize details |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Understand how dental caries develops | Caries develops when cariogenic biofilm bacteria metabolize fermentable carbohydrate over time, producing organic acids that lower plaque pH and dissolve tooth minerals. If demineralization exceeds remineralization repeatedly, an intact subsurface lesion can progress to cavitation. | Host/tooth + saliva, plaque/bacteria, diet/substrate, time; organic acids -> demineralization -> proteolytic breakdown/cavitation. |
| Be knowledgeable of caries pathophysiology | Caries is a dynamic imbalance between demineralization and remineralization. pH fluctuations below the critical level favor mineral loss; saliva, calcium/phosphate, fluoride, and plaque control favor repair. Disease expression depends on risk/protective balance. | Critical pH for enamel: 5.5; demin/remin are continuous; caries balance model. |
| Identify clinical appearance of caries | Early lesions may appear chalky/hyper-white with intact surface; progression can show staining, surface breakdown, cavitation, root-surface soft discolored lesions, or recurrent lesions around restorations. | White spot early demineralization; arrested lesion = shiny/lustrous, brown stain may occur but is not required; root caries = discrete, well-defined, discolored soft area on cementum/root. |
| Be familiar with distribution of dental caries | Distribution can be described by population group, tooth/surface, treated vs untreated disease, total caries experience, and severity indices. Caries increases with age; high-risk groups include lower SES and certain racial/ethnic groups; pit-and-fissure disease has become proportionally more important. | Total caries = treated + untreated; untreated caries not yet treated; DMFT/DMFS = severity/intensity, not prevalence. |
| Know risk factors and risk indicators of caries | Risk increases with visible lesions, recent caries/restorations, teeth missing due to caries, frequent between-meal sugar exposure, low fluoride exposure, visible plaque, exposed roots, appliances, special health care needs, medications reducing saliva, dry mouth, chemo/radiation, eating disorders, and drug/alcohol abuse. Protective factors include saliva, fluoride, oral hygiene, restricted carbohydrate exposure, sugar substitutes, dental home, and regular care. | CRA uses low/moderate/high categories with 0/1/10-point logic; 36-month history matters. |
| Understand the role of fluorides in caries prevention | Fluoride reduces caries by strengthening the remineralization side of the balance and reducing mineral loss during acidic challenges. It acts mainly topically through saliva/plaque/tooth-surface exposure, though systemic fluoridation provides population-level delivery. | Systemic: water, salt, milk, supplements. Topical: toothpaste, rinse, gel, varnish, SDF. Current water fluoridation target: 0.7 ppm. |
| Understand the role of diet in caries | Diet contributes by providing fermentable carbohydrate substrate for biofilm acid production. Frequency, between-meal exposure, and sticky form increase risk; sugar substitutes, especially xylitol-containing products, may reduce cariogenic challenge when used alongside fluoride and hygiene. | Diet = total intake; nutrition = absorption/use of nutrients. Vipeholm: sticky sugar between meals is worst. |
| Demonstrate understanding of caries risk assessment | CRA estimates disease probability from the balance of protective and risk/pathologic factors using history, exam, radiographs, CRA form, and saliva tests when indicated. The output drives recall, bitewing interval, fluoride/product choice, and prevention intensity. | Low 0; moderate 1-9; high 10+; extreme if high risk plus confirmed severe dry mouth/indicated culture logic. Recall/bitewings: low 12/24, moderate 6/18, high 4/12, extreme 3/6 months. |
| Demonstrate familiarity with caries activity tests | Activity/risk tests are adjuncts used when history or symptoms suggest risk, especially salivary flow testing for dry mouth. They do not replace clinical judgment, examination, radiographs, and risk-factor assessment. | Stimulated saliva flow test; dry mouth on form if <0.7 ml/min; bacterial culture may be used in extreme-risk logic. |
| Know efficacy of caries-preventive agents | Fluoride toothpastes, mouthrinses, gels, varnish, and SDF have source-supported caries-preventive roles. Varnish can remineralize early enamel caries; 38% SDF arrests dentine caries; xylitol gum has evidence of caries reduction but systematic reviews call for stronger trials. | 5% NaF varnish; 38% SDF; 5000 ppm prescription fluoride for high/root/xerostomia contexts. |
| Understand application of caries-preventive agents | Product use escalates with risk: low risk uses nutrition counseling and OTC fluoride toothpaste; moderate adds OTC 0.05% NaF rinse and varnish; high substitutes prescription 1.1% NaF/5000 ppm fluoride; extreme adds dry-mouth rinsing/buffers and salivary/dry-mouth management. | Varnish: small amount, aspiration, expectorate, avoid eating/drinking/smoking for 30 min. SDF: warn about black staining and contraindications. |
| Assess clinical cases and provide prevention strategies | Case management should identify risk level, surface, dentition, cavitation, saliva status, fluoride exposure, diet pattern, and ability to maintain hygiene. Choose prevention and non-restorative treatment before surgical restoration when appropriate. | Non-cavitated occlusal: sealant +/- 5% NaF varnish. Non-cavitated approximal/smooth: varnish or resin infiltration options. Cavitated: 38% SDF. Root: 5000 ppm fluoride or SDF. |
| Understand etiology and pathology of caries | Etiology is multifactorial: cariogenic biofilm, fermentable carbohydrate, susceptible host/tooth/saliva, and time. Pathology is repeated acid-mediated mineral dissolution followed by tissue breakdown if the surface integrity fails. | Acidogenic/chemoparasitic theory; Orland/Keys demonstrated bacteria are required; caries can be infectious/transmissible. |
| Recognize stages of caries development | The clinical sequence is normal surface -> decalcification/hyper-white change -> staining/darker surface -> break in surface integrity -> cavitation and possible pulpal/periapical consequences. | White spot can arrest/reverse while surface remains intact; cavitation is later. |
| Know pulpal clinical and histologic changes due to caries | Pulp responds to caries with immune, vascular, neurogenic, and morphologic changes. Bacterial by-products pass through dentinal tubules and cause inflammation before actual bacterial invasion. With progression, increased vascular permeability and tissue pressure create pain and may lead to necrosis/periapical spread. | Pulp is low-compliance, has inefficient lymph drainage in inflammation, and lacks collateral blood supply. |

## Visual Pathways and Mechanisms

### Core Disease Framework
* BIOFILM + FERMENTABLE CARBOHYDRATE + TIME
* -> organic acids in plaque
* -> pH falls below critical range
* -> DEMINERALIZATION (mineral loss)
* -> protective response (saliva Ca/PO4 + fluoride)
* -> balance decides: arrest/remineralize OR progress/cavitate

### Caries Development Sequence
* 1. Biofilm bacteria colonize tooth surface.
* 2. Fermentable carbohydrate becomes available.
* 3. Bacteria produce organic acids.
* 4. Plaque pH falls.
* 5. If pH drops below critical threshold repeatedly, enamel minerals dissolve.
* 6. Saliva/fluoride/calcium/phosphate can remineralize early damage.
* 7. Continued imbalance produces visible white spot, staining, surface breakdown, cavitation, and possible pulpal involvement.

### Stephan Curve Logic
* carbohydrate exposure -> plaque pH drops quickly
* if pH 5.5 -> enamel demineralization favored
* saliva buffering -> clearance slowly raises pH
* repeated frequent exposures = less recovery time = higher caries risk

### Clinical Diagnosis Decision Tree
* clean + dry tooth -> lesion present?
* If no -> routine prevention + recall
* If yes -> active vs arrested?
* cavitated?
* If no -> remineralize / seal / infiltrate
* If yes -> arrest with SDF or restore when indicated

### Pulpal Progression Map
* deepening caries -> bacterial by-products diffuse through dentinal tubules
* pulp inflammation in low-compliance space
* short sharp cold pain -> reversible pulpitis pattern
* lingering/spontaneous pain -> irreversible pulpitis pattern
* loss of vitality/periapical spread -> necrosis pathway

### Risk-to-Recall Ladder
* LOW risk -> recall 12 mo -> bitewings 24 mo
* MODERATE risk -> recall 6 mo -> bitewings 18 mo
* HIGH risk -> recall 4 mo -> bitewings 12 mo
* EXTREME risk -> recall 3 mo -> bitewings 6 mo

### Risk-Based Product Escalation
* LOW: nutrition counseling + OTC fluoride toothpaste 2x/day
* MODERATE: low regimen + OTC 0.05% NaF rinse + 5% NaF varnish
* HIGH: moderate regimen + prescription 1.1% NaF / 5000 ppm toothpaste
* EXTREME: high regimen + dry-mouth rinse/buffers + salivary/bacterial adjuncts as indicated

## Comparison Tables

### Sound / Initial / Cavitated / Arrested Lesions
| Feature | Sound surface | Initial/white spot | Cavitated lesion | Arrested lesion |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Surface integrity | Intact | Intact | Broken | Usually intact/stable surface |
| Appearance | Normal enamel/dentin/cementum | Chalky/hyper-white | Surface breakdown, cavity/decay | Shiny/lustrous; brown stain may occur |
| Disease activity | None | Active or potentially active early demineralization | Advanced active/destructive disease unless arrested | Stopped progression |
| Management logic | Maintain prevention | Remineralize/prevent; monitor | Non-restorative or restorative depending on case | Monitor/prevention; not "reverse to normal" |

### Systemic vs Topical Fluoride
| Feature | Systemic fluoride | Topical fluoride |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Route | Ingested and distributed | Applied/available at tooth surface |
| Examples | Water, salt, milk, supplements | Toothpaste, mouthrinse, gels, varnish, SDF |
| Main exam value | Population prevention; fluorosis balance | Direct caries prevention through plaque/saliva/tooth surface |

### Salivary Flow and Function
| Item | Value / role |
| :--- | :--- |
| Daily saliva | 1-1.5 L |
| pH | 6.0-7.5 |
| Resting unstimulated | 0.3-0.5 ml/min |
| Mechanical stimulated | 1-2 ml/min |
| CRA dry-mouth threshold | <0.7 ml/min by salivary flow test |

### Pulp Pain Fibers
| Feature | A-delta fibers | C fibers |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Myelin | Yes | No |
| Pain quality | Sharp, pricking, cold | Burning, aching, lingering |
| Threshold | Low | High |

### Non-Restorative Treatment Matrix
| Surface/dentition | Non-cavitated | Cavitated |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Occlusal primary/permanent | Sealant + 5% NaF varnish OR sealant alone | 38% SDF |
| Approximal primary/permanent | 5% NaF varnish OR resin infiltration OR both | 38% SDF |
| Facial/lingual primary/permanent | 5% NaF varnish OR 1.23% APF | 38% SDF |
| Root permanent | 5000 ppm/1.1% NaF toothpaste or gel; SDF | 5000 ppm/1.1% NaF toothpaste or gel; SDF |
